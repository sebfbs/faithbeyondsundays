# FBS Referral Program — Research & Strategy
*Synthesized from: Alex Hormozi ($100M Leads), Lenny Rachitsky (Airbnb), Andrew Chen (Uber), David Sacks (Yammer/SaaStr), church tech competitive analysis*

---

## The Answer

**Structure:** 20% recurring monthly commission on active subscriptions, for the lifetime of the referred church's subscription
**Amount:** $100/month per active referred church (20% of $499)
**Framing:** "Refer five churches, your subscription is covered. Every referral after that is pure income."

Every major expert converges on recurring monthly commissions for subscription products. One-time bounties break retention alignment — the referrer cashes out and stops caring. Recurring commissions turn referring pastors into retention partners. If the church they referred churns, their check shrinks. That alignment is everything.

| 1 church  | $100/mo        | $1,200/yr     |                        |
| 3 churches| $300/mo        | $3,600/yr     |                        |

The "FBS for free at 5 referrals" moment is what makes it spread without selling. A pastor at a denomination conference talks to 30 peers — they only need 5 of them to sign up and they never pay for FBS again.

## FBS Margin Check

- Revenue per church: $499/mo
- Your cost (~80% gross margin): ~$100/mo
- Affiliate payout: $100/mo
- **You keep: ~$299/mo per referred church (~60% net margin)**

Sustainable at any scale. Blue Apron's cautionary tale (burned $8M on referral rewards): the safety formula is total lifetime payout should not exceed 30–40% of gross margin per customer. FBS is at 25% of gross margin. Well inside the safe zone.

## Key Rules from the Research

### 1. Referrers must be paying customers (Hormozi)
A pastor paying $499/mo who earns $100 back per referral is a credible, motivated advocate. A free "ambassador account" they've never paid for produces weak, unconvincing referrals. Require active subscription to participate.

> "Nine times out of ten, if they pay, they'll pay attention." — Hormozi

### 2. Make it dual-sided (Lenny Rachitsky — Airbnb)
The referred church should get something too. Dual-sided incentives drive 40–60% higher conversion on the receiving end.
- **Recommendation:** Referred church gets first month free

### 3. Don't launch before product-market fit signals (Lenny + Andrew Chen)
Referral programs amplify existing word-of-mouth. They do not create it. Wait until pastors are already mentioning FBS unprompted — at conferences, in their networks, in group chats. Then launch the program to pour fuel on that fire. Launching cold produces almost nothing.

**Signal to watch for:** Unprompted testimonials. Pastors sharing app screenshots. FBS mentioned at a denominational event without you engineering it.

### 4. Expect a power law (David Sacks — Yammer)
1–3 "super-referring" pastors will drive 70–80% of referral growth. Find them early. Give them direct access to you. Treat them like board members.

### 5. Ask multiple times, in multiple places
Only 11% of SaaS founders ever ask for a referral. Of those, most ask once. Build the ask into:
- The 30-day onboarding check-in
- The 90-day review call
- After any milestone moment ("Your members hit 30-day streaks!" → ask)
- A dedicated tab inside the admin dashboard

### 6. The real-time earnings dashboard is not optional
Every expert cites this as the highest-retention mechanism for referral programs. Every referring pastor should see, on login, exactly how much they've earned this month and this year. Without it, most referrers go passive within 30 days.

## Hormozi's "Affiliate Army" Framework ($100M Leads)

1. **Find your ideal affiliate** — "Who already has my leads?" For FBS: denomination coaches, ARC/C3/Wesleyan network pastors, church consultants, worship leaders who move between churches. They have the trust already.
2. **Make them an offer** — Pay them close to your full CAC. A referred customer LTVs at $499 × 24 months = ~$12,000. Paying $100/month recurring is not generous — it's rational.
3. **Require skin in the game** — Active subscription to participate.
4. **Activate with a launch** — Whisper-tease-shout: start building anticipation 14 days out, increase to twice-weekly at one week, go loud in final 3 days.
5. **Keep them advertising** — Recurring commissions do this automatically. Add a private "FBS Partner" community for top referrers (annual call, status, early access to features).

### The 100% First Month Tactic (Aggressive but powerful)
Hormozi recommends offering 100% of the first month to the introducing partner in some contexts. For FBS, this means: "Refer a church, they stay for month 1, you keep their $499." This is a one-time aggressive acquisition play — only for your first 5–10 launch partners. Costs $499 once, LTV is $12,000. Math works.

### The Referral Ask Script
Instead of asking for a sale, ask:
> "Do you know any pastor who's struggling to keep their congregation engaged between Sundays, and wants to see their people actually living out their faith during the week?"

This is an introduction request, not a sales request. That's a completely different conversation.

## Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Why it kills the program |
|---------|--------------------------|
| Launching before product-market fit | Amplifies nothing; produces nothing |
| Free "ambassador" accounts | Unconvincing referrals with no skin in game |
| One-time bounty on a recurring product | Breaks alignment; referrer cashes out and stops caring |
| Single-sided reward | 40–60% lower conversion on referred church end |
| No real-time earnings dashboard | Referrers go passive within 30 days |
| Asking only once | Most referrals never happen because nobody asked |
| Paying after churn | Commission must stop when referred church churns |
| Tiering too early | Complexity kills participation in a small market |

## Solving the Pastor Guilt Problem

Pastors are in a position of trust. Getting paid to recommend something — even something they genuinely believe in — can feel like a conflict of interest. The fix is transparency, not hiding the money.

### The Core Solution: Give Them Two Options at Signup

> **"Where would you like your referral earnings sent?"**
> - My personal bank account
> - My church/ministry account

That single choice removes almost all the guilt. The guilt comes from secretly profiting. When it's a disclosed, formal option — like a speaking fee or honorarium — most of that discomfort disappears.

### The Framing That Makes Personal Income Feel Clean
Pastors already receive personal income tied to their pastoral role: speaking fees, book royalties, conference honorariums. Position referral commissions in that same category explicitly in onboarding copy:

> *"Your time and endorsement have real value. This is your compensation for it — just like a speaking fee."*

### Additional Features That Kill the Guilt
- **Disclosure toggle** — A one-click option to tell referred churches: "Full transparency: I'm an FBS Partner and earn a referral fee for this introduction." Pastors who disclose stop worrying entirely.
- **1099 at year end** — Legitimate taxable income on a tax form feels like a real professional arrangement, not a kickback. Issue automatically.
- **Split option** — Let pastors route a percentage to their personal account and a percentage to their church/ministry fund. Removes the all-or-nothing pressure entirely.

### The Principle
Both options (personal and church) should feel equally honored in the UI — not like personal = greedy and church = noble. The choice itself is what makes either option feel legitimate.
